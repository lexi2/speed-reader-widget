The plain-text revival

February 14, 2026 · by Sam Mendoza

Plain text is having a quiet renaissance, and the reasons are mostly defensive. People are tired of formatting that breaks across apps, of WYSIWYG editors that smuggle in HTML you cannot see, of documents that become unreadable in three years because the company that made the format is gone.

The plain-text answer to all of this is to use less. Markdown for prose. CSV for tables. JSON or YAML for structure. The files are small, the formats are open, and you can read them with anything that can read a file.

What is interesting is that the appeal has spread beyond programmers. Writers use Markdown because pandoc converts it to anything. Researchers use plain text because git tracks changes well. Note-taking apps like Obsidian and Logseq are popular not because they have the best UX, but because the notes are still files on disk in a format that will outlive the app.

There is a recurring pattern here. People reach for fancy tools, get burned by lock-in or fragility, and retreat to simpler ones. The simpler tools were always there; the experience of needing them is what changed.

None of this is a complete answer. Plain text cannot hold a spreadsheet, or a diagram, or a video. But the surprise is how often it can hold the rest of the work. Most documents are mostly prose. Once you accept that, the case for plain text writes itself.
