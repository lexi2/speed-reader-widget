When the platform you write on becomes the audience you write for

By Lena Brooks · Apr 12, 2026

Every writing platform develops, eventually, a house style. Not a style guide — those are explicit and easily ignored. A house style is what emerges when thousands of people write in the same place, see what gets read, and adjust accordingly without deciding to.

Twitter's house style was the punchline. Medium's was the confessional. The Substack house style, by the time it became one, was the long, patient argument that opens with a digression about something the writer noticed last week.

The mechanism is the same in each case. The platform tilts the playing field toward a particular shape of attention. The writers who succeed there are the ones whose instincts happen to match the tilt. Over time, the tilt becomes invisible to them, because everything they read on the platform was written by people the platform had already selected for.

This is not a complaint. House styles are useful — they let readers and writers calibrate quickly to each other. But it is worth knowing that the style you think you are writing is also, in part, the style the platform has nudged you toward. The writing that breaks out is the writing that ignores the nudge.

The interesting question, then, is not what the house style of your platform happens to be. It is what you would write if you were not aware of one. That is almost always a different essay than the one you started.
