The quiet revolution in how we read

Reading has always been a private act, but the way we do it has changed more in the last decade than in the previous century. Where once we sat with a paper for an hour, we now scan a headline, skim three paragraphs, and move on to the next thing in our feed.

Rapid Serial Visual Presentation, or RSVP, asks a question that feels almost contrarian in this environment: what if you could read faster without skipping anything? Instead of moving your eyes across a line of text, each word is shown in turn, in the same spot, at a pace you choose.

The technique is not new. Researchers have studied it since the 1970s, and the broad result is that most people can comfortably read at 400 to 600 words per minute this way — roughly double their natural pace — without any meaningful loss of comprehension. With practice, some readers push past 800.

The catch is that RSVP works best for prose that you read once, linearly, without needing to skip back. News articles fit this perfectly. Long-form essays often do. Reference material, code, and poetry generally do not.

What makes the modern RSVP renaissance interesting is not the technique itself, but the surface area it now has. A web component dropped into a content management system means the writers do not need to think about it, and the readers can opt in whenever they want to go faster.

That is, in a sense, the whole pitch. Reading should be calm and unhurried when you want it to be, and brisk when you do not. The tools should not get in the way of either choice.
